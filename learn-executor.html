Understanding Estates

What is an executor — and why does it matter?

Most people have never thought about this. Here's everything you need to know, explained simply.

Let's start with the simple version

When you die, someone has to deal with everything you leave behind. Your bank accounts need to be closed. Your bills need to be paid. Your belongings need to go to the right people. Your taxes need to be filed one final time.

That person is called your executor. They're the one who wraps up your life on your behalf.

💡 Think of an executor like a project manager for your estate. They don't necessarily get anything — they just make sure everything gets handled properly and goes to the right people.

What does an executor actually do?

It's more work than most people realize. An executor typically needs to:

The executor's to-do list

1
Locate your will and file it with the court if required (this is called probate)
2
Notify your bank, employer, insurance company, and government agencies like CRA and Service Canada
3
Pay any outstanding debts and final bills
4
File your final tax return — yes, even after death you owe taxes
5
Distribute your belongings and money according to your will
6
Cancel subscriptions, accounts, memberships, and digital services
7
Handle any real estate — selling a home or transferring ownership

This process usually takes 6 to 18 months. It's not a quick job. It requires paperwork, phone calls, patience, and dealing with bureaucracy — all while grieving.

Who should you choose?

Most people name a spouse, an adult child, or a close trusted friend. There's no perfect answer — but there are some good questions to ask yourself:

Good qualities in an executor

✓
Organized and detail-oriented — they'll be dealing with a lot of paperwork
✓
Calm under pressure — this is an emotionally difficult job
✓
Trustworthy — they'll have access to your finances and assets
✓
Available — they need time to actually do this properly
✓
Lives in Canada — dealing with foreign executors adds complexity

⚠️ One important thing: You can name anyone as your executor — but you should tell them first. Finding out you're someone's executor after they've died, with no preparation, is overwhelming. Have the conversation while you can.

What if you don't name one?

If you die without a will — or without naming an executor — the court will appoint one for you. That person is called an administrator and it's usually a family member who applies for the role.

The problem is this process takes time, can cause family conflict, and the person who ends up in charge might not be who you would have chosen. It also costs more money and creates more stress for your family during an already difficult time.

Real scenario

"My dad died without telling anyone where his will was. We knew he had one but couldn't find it for three weeks. During that time nobody could touch his accounts, his mortgage payments almost lapsed, and my sister and I had our first real fight in 20 years. All of that could have been avoided."

Can an executor be paid?

Yes. In Canada, executors are entitled to reasonable compensation — typically around 2.5% to 5% of the estate's value. Most family members waive this, but it's important to know it's an option, especially if you're asking someone to take on a complex estate.

What about a backup executor?

It's smart to name an alternate executor in your will — someone who steps in if your first choice can't or won't do it. Life is unpredictable, and having a backup means your estate won't be left in limbo if something happens to your executor too.

The conversation you need to have

Once you've chosen someone, sit down with them and go through the basics. Tell them where your will is, where your key documents are, what financial accounts exist, and what you'd want them to know. It doesn't have to be a long conversation — but it needs to happen.
