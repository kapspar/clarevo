Role Guide — Executor

What is an executor in Canada — and what do you actually have to do?

Whether you've just been named executor or you're choosing one, this is everything you need to know — the responsibilities, the process, and the liability.

What an executor actually is

When you die, every loose end in your financial life needs to be tied up — bank accounts closed, taxes filed, debts paid, belongings distributed. Someone has to do all of that. That person is your executor.

In most of Canada the role is called an executor, though Quebec uses the term liquidator and some provinces say estate trustee. The function is identical: they are the legal representative of your estate after you die.

The executor's job is not to inherit anything — it's to manage the process. They act as a fiduciary, meaning they're legally obligated to act in the best interests of the estate and its beneficiaries, not their own.

Think of an executor like the project manager of your estate. Their job is to make sure everything gets handled in the right order, paid to the right people, and closed out properly — usually over 6 to 18 months.

Your legal responsibilities as executor

If you've been named executor, here is what you are legally responsible for. This list is more involved than most people expect.

Core executor duties

1
Locate the will and confirm you are named executor
2
Apply for probate if required (court validation of the will)
3
Obtain death certificates — you will need multiple certified copies
4
Notify all relevant institutions — banks, CRA, Service Canada, insurance companies, employer
5
Create a complete inventory of the deceased's assets and liabilities
6
Open an estate bank account to collect estate funds and pay expenses
7
Pay all outstanding debts — credit cards, loans, mortgages, bills
8
File the final T1 income tax return for the year of death
9
Obtain a CRA clearance certificate before distributing any assets
10
Distribute assets to beneficiaries according to the will
11
Cancel subscriptions, memberships, and digital accounts
12
Handle any real estate — transfer or sell property
13
Keep detailed records of every transaction and decision you make

This is not a weekend project. For a moderately complex estate, expect to spend 100–200 hours over 12–18 months. For estates with real property, a business, or complicated family dynamics, it can be significantly more.

Most of that time is spent finding things, not doing things. An executor who doesn't know where the accounts are, where the will is stored, or what insurance policies exist can spend months doing detective work on a life that's already ended. A documented plan changes that entirely — they start with a complete picture instead of a blank page.

Your personal liability — the part most people don't know

This is the section most executor guides skip, and it's the most important one to understand before you accept the role.

As executor, you can be held personally liable for mistakes. This is not theoretical. Common situations where executors face personal liability:

!
Distributing assets before taxes are paid If you pay out to beneficiaries and then discover the estate owes more tax than is left, you — not the beneficiaries — may be on the hook to the CRA for the shortfall.
!
Missing the CRA clearance certificate The clearance certificate is the CRA's sign-off that all taxes are paid. Distributing the estate before receiving it is one of the most common and costly executor mistakes.
!
Failing to notify creditors properly Most provinces require executors to publish a notice to creditors. If a creditor surfaces after you've distributed the estate, you may be personally responsible.
!
Acting in your own interest If you benefit personally from a decision you make as executor — buying estate property at below market value, for example — beneficiaries can sue you for breach of fiduciary duty.

You can decline the role of executor. If you're named in a will but don't want to take it on, you can formally renounce the executorship before taking any action. Once you start acting as executor, renouncing becomes much more complicated.

The step-by-step process

Here's how the executor process typically unfolds in Canada, broken into phases. Every estate is different, but this is the general sequence.

First 48–72 hours
1
Confirm the death and obtain the death certificate The funeral home issues the death certificate. Order at least 10 certified copies — every institution requires an original. You cannot substitute a photocopy.
2
Locate the will Check the deceased's filing cabinet, safety deposit box, with their lawyer, or in any digital vault they maintained. If you can't find a will, the estate is treated as intestate.
3
Secure the assets Make sure the home is locked, vehicles are secured, and nothing is removed from the estate until you have legal authority to act. Keep a record of the estate's condition.
First 1–4 weeks
4
Notify institutions and government Contact banks, investment firms, insurance companies, Service Canada (to cancel CPP/OAS and apply for the $2,500 CPP Death Benefit), the employer, and the CRA.
5
Apply for probate if required Probate validates the will and grants you legal authority to act on the estate. Most banks and land registries require it before transferring assets held solely in the deceased's name.
6
Open an estate bank account All estate funds should flow through a dedicated account. This keeps estate money separate from your personal funds and makes record-keeping clean and defensible.
7
Create a complete asset and liability inventory List every bank account, investment, property, vehicle, and valuable asset. List every debt. This becomes the foundation for everything that follows.
Months 1–6
8
Pay all debts and liabilities Publish a notice to creditors (required in most provinces), then settle credit cards, loans, mortgages, and outstanding bills from the estate account. Do not distribute to beneficiaries yet.
9
File the final T1 income tax return Due April 30 of the following year, or 6 months after the date of death — whichever is later. This is where the deemed disposition tax is calculated on the estate's assets.
10
Request a CRA clearance certificate This is the CRA's confirmation that all taxes have been paid. Do not distribute the estate without it. Processing typically takes 6–8 weeks after filing.
Final phase
11
Distribute assets to beneficiaries Once the clearance certificate is received and all debts paid, you can distribute assets according to the will. Get signed receipts from each beneficiary.
12
Prepare a final accounting Document every transaction, payment, and distribution. Beneficiaries are entitled to a full accounting. Keep all records for at least 6 years after the estate is closed.
13
Close the estate account and wind up the estate Once everything is settled and beneficiaries have signed off, close the estate bank account. The estate is now formally wound up.

Probate explained

Probate is the court process that validates the will and gives the executor legal authority to act on behalf of the estate. Without it, banks and land registries will often refuse to transfer assets held solely in the deceased's name.

Not every estate needs to go through probate — and for many Canadian families, it can be avoided or significantly reduced with the right planning. The key is which assets are involved. See our full guide: Do you actually need probate — and how do you avoid it?

As executor, the practical question is which assets in the estate actually require probate. Assets that typically bypass probate entirely:

What bypasses probate

✓
Registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA, RRIF, LIRA) with a named beneficiary
✓
Life insurance policies with a named beneficiary
✓
Jointly owned property with right of survivorship (passes directly to surviving owner)
✓
Joint bank accounts with right of survivorship
✓
In Quebec — a notarial will does not require probate

Assets held solely in the deceased's name with no named beneficiary — non-registered investment accounts, real estate, sole bank accounts — will generally need to go through probate before you can transfer them. Most financial institutions and land registries won't act without it.

Probate can take 4 to 8 weeks in straightforward cases. Contested wills or complex estates can take significantly longer — sometimes years. This is why the estate process often stretches well past 12 months.

The final tax return — and why it surprises people

As executor, you must file a final T1 income tax return on behalf of the deceased. This is not optional. And it's often more complex than a regular tax return.

In Canada, the tax rules treat death as a deemed disposition — meaning the government considers the deceased to have sold all of their assets the day before they died. Any unrealized capital gains are triggered and taxable in the year of death.

The RRSP is the biggest shock. Unlike other assets where only the gain is taxed, the entire RRSP balance is treated as income in the year of death and taxed at the top marginal rate. A $300,000 RRSP can generate a tax bill of over $129,000 — all due from the estate before any distribution to beneficiaries.

Asset type How it's taxed on death Can bypass probate?
RRSP / RRIF Full balance taxed as income at marginal rate Yes, with named beneficiary
TFSA Tax-free — no tax on death Yes, with named beneficiary
Non-registered investments Capital gains on unrealized gains only No — goes through probate
Principal residence Tax-free — principal residence exemption applies No — goes through probate
Cottage / second property Capital gains on full unrealized gain No — goes through probate
Life insurance Tax-free to named beneficiary Yes, with named beneficiary

After filing the final return, you must apply to the CRA for a clearance certificate. This document confirms that all taxes have been paid and the CRA has no further claim against the estate. Do not distribute assets without it — if taxes are subsequently assessed, you are personally on the hook.

When there's no will

If the deceased died without a will — called dying intestate — there is no named executor. Someone must apply to the court to become the estate administrator. This is usually a surviving spouse or adult child, but it requires a court application and approval.

Without a will
  • Court appoints an administrator
  • Province decides who inherits
  • Higher probate costs
  • Settlement takes significantly longer
  • Family conflict is far more common
  • No executor prep or documented plan
With a will
  • Named executor has immediate authority
  • Distribution follows deceased's wishes
  • Tax planning was possible in advance
  • Typically settled in 6–12 months
  • Clearer process, less family friction
  • Executor was prepared and briefed

In Ontario, if you die intestate with a spouse and children, your spouse receives the first $350,000 and the remainder is divided equally between the spouse and children. This formula may not reflect your actual wishes — and it creates real conflict when families don't agree.

How to choose an executor

Most people name a spouse, an adult child, or a close trusted friend. There's no perfect choice — but there are qualities that matter more than most people realize.

What to look for in an executor

✓
Organized — they'll be managing paperwork, deadlines, and institutions simultaneously
✓
Financially literate — they need to understand tax returns, probate, and asset distribution
✓
Available — this role takes significant time over 12–18 months. Don't name someone whose life circumstances make this impractical
✓
Lives in Canada — foreign executors create significant legal and tax complications
✓
Can handle family dynamics — if beneficiaries disagree, the executor must navigate that without taking sides
✓
Has agreed to do it — this is the one people skip most often. Tell them while you can.

Always name an alternate executor in your will. If your primary executor predeceases you, is incapacitated, or declines the role, a named alternate ensures the estate doesn't end up in court to appoint someone.

A common situation

"My brother named me executor of his estate. I found out when the lawyer called me, three days after he died. I had no idea where his accounts were, whether he had insurance, or what passwords he used. It took me four months just to figure out what he owned. He could have told me any of this in a single conversation."

Executor compensation

Executors are entitled to reasonable compensation in Canada — this is not charity work. The generally accepted range is 2.5% to 5% of the estate's gross value, depending on the complexity of the work. For a $1 million estate, that's $25,000 to $50,000.

Most executors who are close family members waive this. But if the estate is complex, the process lengthy, or the executor is not a direct beneficiary, compensation is not only appropriate — it's fair.

Executor compensation is taxable income to the executor. The estate can deduct it as an administration expense. If you're also a beneficiary, there may be tax reasons to take the compensation as an inheritance instead — talk to an accountant.

When to hire a lawyer

Not every estate requires a lawyer. A simple estate — clear will, no real property, cooperative family — can often be handled without one. But there are situations where professional help is worth every dollar.

Hire an estate lawyer when:

!
The estate includes real property (especially in high-probate provinces like Ontario or BC)
!
There is no will or the will is being contested
!
There are minor children or a dependent who needs ongoing financial provisions
!
The estate includes a business or shares of a private corporation
!
There are creditors making claims against the estate
!
Beneficiaries are in conflict
!
You're unsure about your personal liability exposure

Estate lawyers typically charge either a flat fee for probate applications or a percentage of the estate (usually 1–3%). The cost of a lawyer is an estate expense — it comes out of the estate, not your pocket.
