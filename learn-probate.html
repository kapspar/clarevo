Estate Planning

Do you actually need probate — and how do you avoid it?

Not every Canadian estate needs to go through probate. Here's what determines whether you need it, and how proper planning can reduce or eliminate the cost entirely.

What probate actually is

Probate is a court process that does two things: it confirms your will is valid, and it gives your executor the legal authority to act on behalf of your estate. The result is a document called a Certificate of Appointment of Estate Trustee — in Ontario and most provinces — which banks and land registries accept as proof that your executor has the right to transfer assets.

The court charges a fee for this. In Ontario it's approximately 1.5% of the estate's value. In Alberta it's capped at $525. In Nova Scotia it can reach $168,000 on a $10 million estate. The same estate, a different province, a wildly different bill.

Probate fees are based on your province of domicile — where you permanently lived — not where you happened to die. An Ontario resident who dies on a trip to Alberta still pays Ontario probate rates.

When you do — and don't — need probate

This is where most guides are unclear. Probate is not legally mandatory in Canada — but in practice, most financial institutions and land registries won't transfer certain assets without it. The real question is which assets require it and which don't.

The rule of thumb: any asset held solely in your name, with no named beneficiary and no joint owner, will generally require probate before it can be transferred. Everything else can often bypass it.

Asset type Probate required? Why
RRSP / RRIF with named beneficiary No Pays directly to beneficiary, bypasses estate
TFSA with named beneficiary or successor No Transfers directly outside the estate
Life insurance with named beneficiary No Death benefit paid directly to beneficiary
Jointly owned home (right of survivorship) No Passes directly to surviving joint owner
Joint bank accounts No Surviving account holder retains access
RRSP / RRIF with no named beneficiary Yes Falls into estate, goes through probate
Non-registered investment accounts (sole) Yes Can't name beneficiaries on regular accounts in Canada
Real estate in sole name Yes Land registry requires probate to transfer title
Bank accounts in sole name (large balance) Often Institution's discretion — many require probate over certain thresholds
Notarial will in Quebec No Registered with provincial notary — court validation not required

Even for assets that technically don't require probate, some institutions will ask for it anyway — particularly for large account balances. "Not required" means you have a strong argument, not a guarantee. The smaller and simpler the estate, the more likely you can avoid it.

How to avoid or minimize probate with planning

The goal of probate planning isn't to hide assets from the government — it's to structure your estate so that as few assets as possible need to pass through the court process. Every dollar that bypasses probate reaches your family faster and costs them nothing in fees.

Here are the main strategies, roughly in order of impact for most Canadian families.

1

Name beneficiaries on all registered accounts

This is the single highest-impact action most Canadians can take. Your RRSP, TFSA, RRIF, LIRA, pension, and life insurance all allow you to name a beneficiary directly. When you die, those assets pay out straight to the named person — completely outside the estate, no probate, no waiting.

Many people have registered accounts with no beneficiary named, or with an outdated one from a previous relationship. This is one of the most common gaps the Clarevo assessment surfaces — and one of the cheapest to fix. A 10-minute call to your financial institution is all it takes.

Highest impact
2

Use successor designations on TFSAs and RRIFs

For spouses specifically, a successor holder (TFSA) or successor annuitant (RRIF) designation is even cleaner than a regular beneficiary. Instead of the plan collapsing and the money being paid out, your spouse simply takes over the account intact — maintaining its tax status, no tax triggered, seamless transfer.

If you're married or in a common-law relationship and haven't set this up, it's worth a conversation with your financial institution this week.

High impact for spouses
3

Hold property jointly with right of survivorship

Property held jointly with right of survivorship passes directly to the surviving owner without touching the estate. For most married couples, the family home is already held this way — but it's worth confirming. Check your property deed or land title to verify how ownership is recorded.

Note that joint ownership is not always the right move. Adding an adult child to the title of your home — a popular but risky strategy — can trigger capital gains, create creditor exposure, and jeopardize the principal residence exemption. See the risks section below before pursuing this.

Medium impact — verify existing assets
4

Use a secondary will for private business shares

If you own shares of a private corporation and live in Ontario or BC — two high-probate provinces — a secondary will strategy can be valuable. The idea is to have two separate wills: one for assets that need to go through probate, and a second for assets that don't (like private company shares). The second will doesn't get probated, so no fee applies to those assets.

This is a lawyer conversation — it needs to be structured carefully to be effective — but for business owners in Ontario or BC with significant private company holdings, the savings can be substantial.

Specific to business owners in ON/BC
5

Use a notarial will if you live in Quebec

Quebec operates under civil law rather than common law, which means the rules are different. A notarial will — drafted and signed before a notary — is registered in the provincial notary registry and doesn't require probate at all. If you live in Quebec and don't have a notarial will, this is the most direct path to avoiding the process entirely.

Quebec residents only
6

Gift assets while you're alive

Assets you give away during your lifetime aren't part of your estate when you die — so they don't attract probate fees. For families where it makes financial sense, transferring assets to adult children while you're alive (called inter vivos gifting) can reduce the taxable estate.

The catch: gifting non-cash assets can trigger capital gains tax at the time of the gift, not just at death. And you need to make sure your own retirement is secure before giving anything away. This is a strategy to model carefully with a financial advisor, not something to do casually.

Requires financial planning first

The risks of DIY probate avoidance

Some strategies that circulate — often passed around as tips between friends or family — can do real damage if done without proper advice. These are the ones worth being cautious about.

Common mistakes that backfire

!
Adding an adult child to the title of your home. If your child owns their own home, adding them to yours means one of you loses the principal residence exemption. You've potentially saved $15,000 in probate but created a $50,000+ capital gains problem. Their divorce, debt, or financial trouble also now puts your home at risk.
!
Adding a child to a bank account. Same issue — it exposes the account to their creditors and creates a legal grey area around whether it's a gift or a convenience arrangement. Courts have gone both ways on this.
!
Naming a minor as beneficiary. Children under 18 can't legally receive large sums directly. If a minor is named as beneficiary on an RRSP or life insurance policy, the funds will be paid to the Office of the Public Guardian and Trustee — essentially into government custody — until the child turns 18. Use the will to set up a trust instead.
!
Forgetting to update beneficiary designations after major life changes. A divorce, remarriage, or death in the family can leave an ex-spouse or deceased person as beneficiary on a registered account. That money will still go to them — or their estate — regardless of what your will says.

The cost of getting probate planning wrong is often higher than the cost of doing nothing. Before making any changes to property ownership or beneficiary designations, talk to a financial advisor or estate lawyer who can model the full tax and legal impact.
