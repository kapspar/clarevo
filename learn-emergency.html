Emergency Planning

What happens in a family financial emergency?

Nobody wants to think about this. But knowing what to do — before it happens — is one of the most loving things you can do for your family.

Let's be honest about why this matters

Imagine this: the person in your household who handles the finances is suddenly in the hospital. Or they've passed away. Or they're completely unreachable for an extended period.

What happens next? Who pays the mortgage? Who knows which bills are on autopay? Who can access the bank accounts? Who knows where the insurance documents are? Who do you call first?

For most families, the honest answer is: nobody knows. And that uncertainty turns a crisis into chaos.

💡 This page isn't meant to scare you. It's meant to help you feel prepared. Knowing what to expect — and having a plan — makes everything more manageable.

The first 72 hours

The first three days after a financial emergency are the most disorienting. Here's a realistic picture of what needs to happen and when:

1
Within hours

Notify immediate family

Make sure the right people know what's happening. This sounds obvious but in the shock of the moment, people freeze. Have a short list of who to call first.

2
Day 1

Identify urgent bills

Mortgage payments, car payments, and utilities have grace periods — but you need to know what's coming due. If bills are documented somewhere, this is easy. If not, this is where families struggle most.

3
Day 1–2

Access emergency funds

You'll need money for immediate expenses. If your partner has access to a joint account or knows the banking information, this is straightforward. If not, this becomes a serious problem quickly.

4
Day 2–3

Notify the employer

If the person is hospitalized or has passed away, their employer needs to know. This affects pay, benefits, and any life insurance through work.

5
Day 3–7

Contact the lawyer

If someone has passed away, a lawyer needs to be involved to handle the estate. If you don't know who their lawyer is, or where the will is, this step gets significantly harder.

6
Week 2 onward

Notify financial institutions

Banks, investment accounts, insurance companies, and government agencies all need to be contacted. This is where having an organized list of accounts makes an enormous difference.

What you actually need to know — and where to find it

When a crisis hits, your family needs to find specific information quickly. Here's what matters most:

🏦

Banking information

Which banks you use, what types of accounts exist, and how to access them. Not full account numbers — just enough to know where to call.

🧾

Monthly bills list

What's due, when it's due, and whether it's on autopay. The mortgage, utilities, insurance premiums, subscriptions that need to be cancelled.

📄

Will and legal documents

Where is the will? Who is the lawyer? Who is the executor? These questions need instant answers — not a search through filing cabinets.

🛡️

Insurance policies

Life insurance company, policy number, how to make a claim. This is often worth hundreds of thousands of dollars — and families miss it because they don't know it exists.

🔐

Password access

Email, banking apps, online accounts. If everything is locked behind a password only one person knows, your family is locked out of their own life.

👶

Kids' logistics

School, daycare, doctor, allergies, emergency contacts, after-school routines. Who picks them up? Who do you call?

The difference between prepared and unprepared

Unprepared family

"We didn't know which bank his RRSP was at. We didn't know where the will was. We found out about a life insurance policy three months after he died — from a piece of mail. His mortgage went into arrears for 30 days while we tried to figure out the automatic payment. It was the hardest three months of my life and half of it didn't have to be that hard."

Prepared family

"She had everything in one place. I knew exactly who to call, where the documents were, what bills were coming due. The grief was still crushing — but I never felt lost. I could focus on the kids and on healing instead of on paperwork and panic."

What about when someone is hospitalized but alive?

This situation is actually more common than death — and often harder to navigate financially. If your partner is in the ICU, they can't sign cheques, they can't call the bank, they can't manage anything.

Without a Power of Attorney for Property, you may not have legal authority to access their accounts, pay their bills, or make financial decisions on their behalf — even as their spouse.

⚠️ Many people are surprised to learn that being married doesn't automatically give you legal authority over your spouse's finances if they're incapacitated. A Power of Attorney is the document that does. Without it, you may need a court order — and that takes time and money you don't have during a crisis.

Who to call — a practical contact list

Key contacts to have documented

🏦 Your bank's emergency line — most have a 24/7 number for account access issues
🛡️ Your life insurance company — policy number and claims number
⚖️ Your lawyer — especially the one who holds your will
💼 Your employer's HR department — for benefits and group insurance
🏛️ Service Canada — 1-800-277-9914 — for CPP death benefit and survivor's pension
📋 Canada Revenue Agency — final tax return and estate matters
🏠 Your mortgage lender — most have mortgage life insurance or payment deferral options

The one thing you can do right now

You don't need to have everything perfectly organized today. But you do need to start. The single most important thing you can do is make sure someone other than you knows where things are.

Tell your partner — or someone you trust — where your will is, what bank you use, and where your insurance documents are. That conversation takes ten minutes and it could save your family weeks of stress.

Your emergency readiness checklist

✓
Someone knows where your will is stored
✓
Someone knows which banks and financial institutions you use
✓
Your monthly bills are documented somewhere findable
✓
Your life insurance company and policy number are documented
✓
Your executor knows they're your executor
✓
Someone has access to your passwords or knows how to get them
✓
Your kids' school, doctor, and emergency contacts are written down
✓
You have a Power of Attorney in place
