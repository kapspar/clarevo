Digital Legacy

What happens to your phone, photos, and online accounts?

Your whole life is on your devices now. Here's how to make sure your family can actually reach it.

The problem nobody thinks about

A generation ago, everything important was on paper in a drawer. Today it's behind a passcode on your phone and a password on a dozen accounts. Years of family photos, your email, your messages, even access to your banking — all locked behind credentials only you know.

When someone passes away, families often discover they can't get into the one device that holds every photo of their kids growing up. Not because anyone did anything wrong — just because nobody set it up ahead of time.

💡 The good news: both Apple and Google have built tools specifically for this. They take ten minutes to set up and they're free. Most people just don't know they exist.

Apple: Legacy Contact

An Apple Legacy Contact is a trusted person you choose who can access your Apple Account data after you pass away. They can download photos, messages, and documents — though not sensitive things like saved passwords or purchased media.

It means your loved ones can reach your digital memories without going through a slow, expensive court process.

How to set up an Apple Legacy Contact

1
On iPhone or iPad: open Settings, tap your name at the top, then Sign-In & Security, then Legacy Contact
2
On a Mac: open the Apple menu, then System Settings, your name, Sign-In & Security, then Legacy Contact
3
Choose the person you trust — Apple generates a unique access key
4
Send them the key (it can go straight to them in Messages), or print it and keep it with your estate documents

Google: Inactive Account Manager

Google's version is called Inactive Account Manager. You decide how long your account can sit unused — say three or six months — and who should be notified or given access if you never sign back in. You choose exactly which data they can see, from photos to email to Drive.

How to set up Google Inactive Account Manager

1
Go to your Google Account settings, then Data & privacy
2
Find "Make a plan for your account" and open Inactive Account Manager
3
Set the waiting period before the account is considered inactive
4
Add up to ten trusted contacts and choose what each can access

Don't forget your photos

For many families, the photos hurt the most to lose. They might live in iCloud, Google Photos, Amazon Photos, or on an external drive in a closet. Wherever they are, someone needs to know where to look and how to get in.

⚠️ Storing the actual passwords anywhere — even with a lawyer — is risky. The safer approach is to record where recovery information lives (a password manager, a sealed envelope, a legacy contact) rather than the passwords themselves. That's exactly how Clarevo is built to work.

What to actually do this week

Your digital legacy checklist

✓
Set up an Apple Legacy Contact and/or Google Inactive Account Manager
✓
Note where your phone passcode can be found in an emergency
✓
Write down which service holds your photos — and who can reach them
✓
Consider a password manager so one trusted recovery method covers everything
✓
Record all of this in your Clarevo plan under Digital & Devices
