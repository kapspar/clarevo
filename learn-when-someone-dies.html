Crisis Guide

What to do when someone dies in Canada

A plain-English walkthrough of every step — from the first phone call to closing the estate. No jargon, no overwhelm.

If you're reading this right now because someone you love has just died, we're sorry. This guide exists to make an impossibly hard time a little less chaotic.
← Back to all guides

Settling an estate in Canada is not a single phone call. It's a sequence of steps that unfolds over days, weeks, and months — involving doctors, funeral homes, courts, banks, the CRA, and more. Most families figure this out in real time while grieving. This guide lays it out in order so you don't have to.

Who this guide is for: Anyone who has just lost a family member, or is helping someone navigate a loss. It's also useful reading before a death happens — knowing the process is one of the most practical things you can do for your family.
Within 24–48 hours
Immediate steps
1

A doctor confirms the death

A physician (or in some cases a paramedic or coroner) must confirm and register the death. If the person died at home, call 911. If in hospital, the attending physician handles this. This confirmation is what sets the entire legal process in motion.

2

Contact a funeral home

The funeral home takes custody of the body, arranges burial or cremation, and — crucially — issues the Death Certificate. You'll need multiple certified copies (typically 5–10) because every institution requires an original. The funeral home can order these for you, or you can request them from the provincial vital statistics office.

💡 Get at least 10 certified copies of the death certificate. Banks, insurance companies, and government agencies each require originals — they won't accept photocopies.
3

Locate the will and identify the executor

The will names the executor — the person legally responsible for managing the estate. Common places to look: a home filing cabinet, a safety deposit box, with the deceased's lawyer, or in a digital document vault. If there's no will, the estate is settled according to provincial law (called intestate succession), and a court appoints an administrator.

💡 This is exactly the problem Clarevo solves while people are still alive — documenting where the will is, who the executor is, and what accounts exist so families aren't searching in a crisis.
↓
First week
Notifications and accounts

With death certificates in hand, the executor begins notifying institutions. This is often the most time-consuming part — every institution has its own process.

4

Notify the federal government

Contact Service Canada (1-800-277-9914) to cancel CPP/OAS payments, apply for the CPP Death Benefit (a one-time $2,500 payment to the estate), and begin survivor's pension applications if applicable. Also notify the CRA by filing a notification of death.

💡 CPP/OAS payments deposited after the date of death must be returned. Do this promptly to avoid a repayment demand later.
5

Cancel provincial ID and benefits

Contact the provincial health authority to cancel the health card (OHIP in Ontario, CareCard in BC, etc.). Also cancel the driver's licence with the provincial Ministry of Transportation, and the passport with Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (1-800-567-6868).

6

Notify banks and financial institutions

Contact each bank where the deceased held accounts. Bring a certified death certificate. Joint accounts typically remain accessible to the surviving account holder. Sole accounts are frozen until the estate is settled. The executor will be granted access once they have legal authority (the Certificate of Appointment).

7

Notify insurance companies

File claims with all life insurance providers. If beneficiaries are named, those funds pay out directly — bypassing the estate entirely. Also cancel health, dental, auto, and home insurance (or transfer coverage as needed). Check for any workplace group benefits that may include life insurance.

8

Notify the employer

If the deceased was employed, notify HR immediately. You may be entitled to unpaid wages, accrued vacation pay, group insurance proceeds, or pension plan benefits. Ask for a Record of Employment if applicable.

9

Cancel subscriptions and digital accounts

Cancel recurring payments (credit cards, streaming, utilities, phone plan) to stop charges. For digital accounts, most platforms (Apple, Google, Facebook) have legacy contact or memorialization processes. Accessing email and phones often requires passwords — this is why documenting digital access while alive matters so much.

↓
Weeks to months
The estate process
10

Apply for probate (if required)

Probate is the court process that validates the will and grants the executor legal authority to act. It's required before banks and land registries will transfer assets held solely in the deceased's name. The executor applies to the provincial court, and the court charges a fee based on the estate's value.

💡 Registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA, RRIF) and jointly owned assets can bypass probate entirely if beneficiaries are properly named — one of the biggest reasons to set up beneficiary designations while you're alive.
11

File the final tax return

The executor must file a final T1 income tax return for the year of death, due by April 30 of the following year (or 6 months after the date of death, whichever is later). This is where the deemed disposition tax is triggered — the CRA treats all assets as if they were sold the day before death, creating a tax bill on any unrealized gains. The RRSP/RRIF balance is also fully taxed as income in the year of death.

💡 On a $300,000 RRSP at a 53% marginal rate, the tax bill can exceed $129,000 in the year of death alone. This is often the biggest surprise families face.
12

Pay debts and liabilities

Before distributing any assets to beneficiaries, the executor must settle all debts: mortgages, credit cards, loans, and outstanding bills. The estate is responsible — beneficiaries cannot be personally forced to pay the deceased's debts (except in rare cases), but the inheritance they receive is reduced by whatever the estate owes.

13

Distribute assets to beneficiaries

Once taxes are paid, debts settled, and the estate is cleared by the CRA (via a clearance certificate), the executor distributes the remaining assets according to the will. If there's no will, distribution follows provincial intestate succession rules — which may not match what the deceased would have wanted.

What the costs actually look like

Most families are surprised by the size of the tax bill. In Canada, when someone dies, the government treats it as if they sold all their assets the day before — triggering capital gains tax and income tax on registered accounts. Then probate fees are layered on top.

Example: Jamie's estate (Ontario) — $2.3M total

RRSP ($300K) — taxed as income at ~53% $129,990
Non-registered account — capital gains on $50K $10,833
Cottage — capital gains on $200K gain $43,330
Probate fees (Ontario, ~1.5% on eligible assets) $28,500
Total cost of dying $212,653
Source: Wealthsimple Wealth Management webinar. Assumptions: Ontario probate at 1.5%, marginal tax rate 53.53%. TFSA and principal residence are tax-free and not included in the taxable calculation.
The RRSP surprise: The RRSP is often the biggest shock. Unlike other assets where only the gains are taxed, the entire RRSP balance is treated as income in the year of death — at the top marginal rate. A $300,000 RRSP can trigger a tax bill of nearly $130,000, all due in a single year.
Probate fees vary dramatically by province. In Alberta, the fee is capped at $525 regardless of estate size. In BC, Nova Scotia, or Ontario, the same estate can owe over $150,000 in probate fees alone. See the province-by-province breakdown on our Learn hub.

With a will vs. without one

Having a will doesn't just decide who gets what — it determines how hard the entire process is for the people you leave behind.

✓ With a will
  • Executor named and ready to act
  • Distribution follows your wishes
  • Estate typically settled in 6–12 months
  • Tax and probate planning is possible
  • Reduces family conflict significantly
✗ Without a will
  • Court appoints an administrator
  • Province decides who gets what
  • Settlement can take years
  • Higher probate costs, more red tape
  • Family conflict is far more common
The Ontario intestate formula: If you die without a will in Ontario and have a spouse and children, your spouse receives the first $350,000, and the remainder is divided equally between the spouse and children. This may not reflect what you intended — and it creates friction at the worst possible time.

The one thing that makes all of this easier

Everything in this guide becomes significantly harder when the executor doesn't know where to look. No one documents their accounts, passwords, insurance policies, and legal documents in one accessible place — until they're forced to.

According to a 2023 Angus Reid Institute study, 50% of Canadians don't have a will — including 1 in 5 Canadians aged 55 or older. The administrative burden of an undocumented estate falls entirely on the people already dealing with grief.
