Understanding Estates

What is a will — and what actually happens if you don't have one?

53% of Canadians don't have a will. Here's what that really means for the people you love.

What a will actually is

A will is a legal document that tells the world what you want to happen to your stuff when you die. It names who gets your money, your home, your car, your savings, your belongings — and who's responsible for making all of that happen.

It also lets you name a guardian for your children if they're minors. Without it, a court decides that for you.

💡 A will isn't about being wealthy. It's about being clear. It protects your family from having to guess what you would have wanted — at the worst possible time.

What's actually in a will?

A typical will includes

1
Your executor — the person responsible for carrying out your wishes
2
Your beneficiaries — who gets what. Your spouse, children, family, friends, charities
3
Specific gifts — that watch, the car, the cottage — things you want specific people to have
4
Guardian for minor children — who raises your kids if both parents are gone
5
Residual estate — who gets everything that's left after specific gifts and debts are handled
6
Your funeral wishes — burial, cremation, ceremony preferences

What happens if you die without one?

Dying without a will is called dying intestate. When that happens, the government steps in with a set of rules called intestacy laws that decide where your money goes. These rules don't know your situation, your relationships, or what you would have wanted.

🚨 Intestacy laws follow a strict formula — usually spouse first, then children, then other relatives. This sounds fine until you realize it ignores common-law partners in many provinces, step-children, close friends, charities you cared about, and any specific wishes you had.

Real scenario

"My uncle lived with his partner for 12 years. He never got around to making a will. When he died, Ontario's intestacy rules didn't recognize common-law partners at the time. Everything went to his estranged brother — someone he hadn't spoken to in a decade. His partner of 12 years got nothing."

Common myths about wills

❌ Myth

I'm young and healthy — I don't need a will yet.

✓ Reality

Accidents happen at any age. If you have a partner, children, a home, or any assets at all, you need a will. The right time to make one is before you need it.

❌ Myth

I don't have much money so it doesn't matter.

✓ Reality

A will isn't just about money. It's about your children's guardian, your personal belongings, your digital accounts, and protecting the people you love from unnecessary conflict and stress.

❌ Myth

My spouse will automatically get everything.

✓ Reality

Not necessarily. In many Canadian provinces, intestacy laws split your estate between your spouse and your children — meaning your spouse might only get a portion. A will ensures your spouse is fully protected.

❌ Myth

Making a will is expensive and complicated.

✓ Reality

Online services like Willful make a legally valid Canadian will for under $200 in about an hour. You don't need a lawyer for a straightforward estate — though one is worth it for complex situations.

What about beneficiaries on registered accounts?

This is one of the most overlooked things in estate planning. Your TFSA, RRSP, RRIF, and life insurance don't automatically go through your will — they pass directly to whoever you've named as beneficiary on the account.

That means if you named your ex-spouse as beneficiary on your RRSP 10 years ago and never updated it, your ex-spouse gets that money — regardless of what your will says.

⚠️ Review your beneficiary designations every few years, and definitely after any major life change — marriage, divorce, new children, death of a beneficiary. This is one of the most common and costly oversights in estate planning.

When should you update your will?

A will isn't a one-time thing. Life changes and your will should too. Review it after:

Update your will when

✓
You get married or divorced
✓
You have or adopt a child
✓
You buy a home or major asset
✓
Your executor or a beneficiary dies
✓
Your financial situation changes significantly
✓
You move to a different province
✓
It's been more than 5 years since you last reviewed it

How do you make one?

For most Canadians with a straightforward situation — a spouse, kids, a home, registered accounts — an online service is perfectly adequate and much cheaper than a lawyer. Willful is the most well-known Canadian option and creates province-specific wills that are legally valid.

If you have a more complex situation — a business, significant assets, blended family, dependents with special needs — a wills and estates lawyer is worth every dollar.
